Transparency and taxpayer accountability in APCC payments
The Association of Police and Crime Commissioners (APCC) is funded by payments that ultimately come from Thames Valley taxpayers. Yet meaningful transparency regarding where this money goes remains extremely limited. Many residents have raised concerns about value for money and the lack of publicly accessible detailed accounts.

Below is a summary of known APCC spending for the past five years.
Year Amount (£)
202078,000

Conclusion
Matthew Barber has a responsibility to provide full financial transparency.

In our opinion, for transparency Matthew Barber should supply a copy of the full accounts of the APCC for the past 5 years so Thames Valley taxpayers can see how their money is being accounted for. Otherwise, he should arrange for all membership fees to be returned for the use of the Chief Constable of the Thames Valley to employ more officers to investigate crime.

Environmental Crime in South Oxfordshire

"A quarter-million-pound landfill offence – still unprosecuted"
Bulletin #2 will include photographic evidence of a major environmental crime involving an estimated 250 lorries of landfill dumped illegally in South Oxfordshire. This crime is visible on Google Earth, yet neither the Environment Agency nor Oxford County Council have pursued prosecution.

The offence qualifies as fly-tipping—ranging from minor dumping to large-scale illegal waste disposal like the one recently featured in national news (20/11/2025). It would have generated over £250,000 in landfill tax at the highest rate due to toxic waste filling a floodplain. Residents of Cholsey may now face increased flooding risk as a result.

Police Waiting Lists

"What the NHS counts daily — the police say they don't track"
We are all familiar with NHS waiting lists. The NHS knows each day how many beds will be freed and how many patients are waiting.

Matthew Hogg, Chief Constable of the Thames Valley, claims not to know how many reported crimes are awaiting available investigating officers — despite frontline officers describing extremely long internal waiting lists.

A national FOI survey will reveal the real scale of the backlog and provide the first public snapshot of policing “waiting lists”.
